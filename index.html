Visual Lab: Heat Conduction

An interactive virtual laboratory for studying Fourier's Law of Heat Conduction in Plane Walls and Cylindrical Pipes

Aim of the Experiment

This virtual laboratory experiment aims to help engineering students understand and visualize the fundamental principles of heat conduction through different geometries, specifically plane walls and cylindrical pipes, as described by Fourier's Law of Heat Conduction.

Learning Objectives:

  • Understand the mechanism of heat conduction in solids
  • Comprehend Fourier's Law and its mathematical formulation for different geometries
  • Analyze heat conduction through plane walls and cylindrical pipes
  • Calculate heat transfer rates for composite walls and insulated pipes
  • Explore practical applications in engineering systems
  • Understand radial heat flow in cylindrical coordinates

By the end of this experiment, students should be able to predict heat transfer rates through different materials and geometries, and understand the factors affecting thermal conduction in engineering applications such as building insulation, heat exchangers, and pipeline systems.

Theory

Heat conduction is the transfer of thermal energy through a material without any motion of the material as a whole. Fourier's Law provides the fundamental relationship governing heat conduction in different geometries.

Fourier's Law for Plane Walls

Q = -kA(dT/dx)

The rate of heat transfer through a plane wall is proportional to the negative gradient of temperature and the area through which heat flows.

Fourier's Law for Cylindrical Pipes

Q = -kAr(dT/dr)

For cylindrical coordinates, the heat transfer area varies with radius: Ar = 2πrL

Heat Conduction in Plane Walls

In plane walls, heat flows in a straight line perpendicular to the surface. The temperature distribution is linear for steady-state conduction with constant thermal conductivity. This geometry is commonly encountered in building walls, flat plates, and slabs.

Heat Conduction in Cylindrical Pipes

In cylindrical pipes, heat flows radially from the inner surface to the outer surface (or vice versa). The temperature distribution is logarithmic, and the heat transfer area increases with radius. This is crucial for pipeline systems, heat exchangers, and insulated cables.

Comparison of Heat Conduction Geometries

Aspect Plane Wall Cylindrical Pipe
Coordinate System Cartesian (x, y, z) Cylindrical (r, θ, z)
Heat Flow Direction Linear (one-dimensional) Radial (one-dimensional)
Temperature Distribution Linear: T(x) = T₁ + (T₂ - T₁)(x/L) Logarithmic: T(r) = T₁ + (T₂ - T₁)[ln(r/r₁)/ln(r₂/r₁)]
Heat Transfer Area Constant (A) Varies with radius: A(r) = 2πrL
Heat Transfer Rate Q = kA(T₁ - T₂)/L Q = 2πkL(T₁ - T₂)/ln(r₂/r₁)
Thermal Resistance R = L/(kA) R = ln(r₂/r₁)/(2πkL)
Typical Applications Building walls, flat plates, slabs Pipelines, heat exchangers, insulated cables

Heat Conduction Through Cylindrical Pipes

For a long cylindrical pipe with inner radius r₁, outer radius r₂, length L, and constant thermal conductivity k, the heat transfer rate under steady-state conditions is given by:

Q = [2πkL(T₁ - T₂)] / [ln(r₂/r₁)]

Where T₁ is the temperature at inner radius r₁, and T₂ is the temperature at outer radius r₂.

Step-by-Step Calculation for Cylindrical Pipes

1

Identify Known Parameters

Determine inner radius (r₁), outer radius (r₂), pipe length (L), thermal conductivity (k), and temperature difference (T₁ - T₂).

Example:

Steam pipe: r₁ = 0.05 m, r₂ = 0.07 m, L = 10 m
k = 50 W/m·K (steel)
T₁ = 200°C (inner), T₂ = 50°C (outer)

2

Calculate Radius Ratio

Compute the natural logarithm of the radius ratio: ln(r₂/r₁)

Example Calculation:

r₂/r₁ = 0.07 / 0.05 = 1.4
ln(1.4) = 0.336

3

Apply Cylindrical Conduction Formula

Substitute values into: Q = [2πkL(T₁ - T₂)] / [ln(r₂/r₁)]

Example Calculation:

Q = [2π × 50 × 10 × (200-50)] / [ln(0.07/0.05)]
Q = [2π × 50 × 10 × 150] / 0.336
Q = 471,239 / 0.336 = 1,402,000 W = 1.402 MW

4

Interpret the Result

The heat loss from the steam pipe is significant. This highlights the importance of insulation for energy efficiency.

Interpretation:

Heat loss = 1.402 MW from steam to environment
This represents substantial energy loss requiring insulation.

Sample Problem: Insulated Pipe

Problem: A steel pipe (k = 45 W/m·K) with inner radius 50 mm and outer radius 60 mm carries hot fluid at 180°C. The pipe is covered with 40 mm thick insulation (k = 0.1 W/m·K). If the outer surface temperature is 30°C, calculate the heat loss per meter length.

Solution:

  1. Pipe radii: r₁ = 0.05 m, r₂ = 0.06 m
  2. Insulation outer radius: r₃ = 0.06 + 0.04 = 0.10 m
  3. Thermal resistances:
    • Pipe: R₁ = ln(0.06/0.05) / (2π × 45 × 1) = 0.00064 K/W
    • Insulation: R₂ = ln(0.10/0.06) / (2π × 0.1 × 1) = 0.735 K/W
  4. Total resistance: Rtotal = R₁ + R₂ = 0.73564 K/W
  5. Heat loss: Q = ΔT / Rtotal = (180-30) / 0.73564 = 203.9 W/m

Interpretation: The insulation reduces heat loss significantly compared to an uninsulated pipe.

Practical Applications

Pipeline Systems

Cylindrical pipe heat conduction analysis is essential for designing insulation for oil, gas, and steam pipelines to minimize energy loss and prevent freezing or overheating.

Heat Exchangers

Tubular heat exchangers rely on radial heat conduction through pipe walls for efficient heat transfer between fluids in industrial processes.

Electrical Cables

Understanding radial heat conduction helps design proper insulation for electrical cables to prevent overheating and ensure safe operation.

  3. Double the pipe length (L) and recalculate - what happens to Q?

  5. Change thermal conductivity from steel (45 W/m·K) to insulation (0.05 W/m·K) - observe the dramatic change
  6. Observe how the temperature distribution changes from linear to logarithmic
